University of Zurich · Department of Economics · May 2026                                       46
        Princeton econometrics, 1980s




         Joshua Angrist’s recollection: in Princeton’s PhD program, potential outcomes were not taught
         Econometrics was about coefficients — “the structure,” “the causal effect” — not about weighted
         averages of unit-level effects
         The vocabulary that lets us write Yi (1) − Yi (0) was not yet standard in economics




University of Zurich · Department of Economics · May 2026                                                  47
        Rubin (1974) revives Neyman (1923)




         Jerzy Neyman, 1923 dissertation: introduced potential outcomes notation
         Donald Rubin, 1974: “Estimating Causal Effects of Treatments in Randomized and
         Nonrandomized Studies”
         Together they gave us the explicit language of counterfactuals — Yi (1) and Yi (0) as the two
         states of the same unit


     50-year gap. The notation existed before economists started using it.




University of Zurich · Department of Economics · May 2026                                                48
        The individual treatment effect




                                          δi = Yi (1) − Yi (0)

         One per unit. Different across units in general — treatment effects are heterogeneous.
         We only ever see one of Yi (1) or Yi (0). The other is the counterfactual.
         So δi is never directly observed at the individual level.




University of Zurich · Department of Economics · May 2026                                         49
        Three averages of δi — three estimands


     All three are expected values. The only difference is whom we average over :

         ATE = E[δi ]                                                        average over everyone
         ATT = E[δi | Di = 1]                                        average over treated units only
         ATU = E[δi | Di = 0]                                      average over untreated units only



                        ATE = π · ATT + (1 − π) · ATU,      where π = Pr(D = 1).



      With heterogeneous effects, the three are different numbers. The one you want depends on the
                                                 question.

        Which causal effect do you want? Weighting decides.




     Solon, Haider & Wooldridge (2015), What Are We Weighting For?

         In a survey, you weight to make estimates nationally representative.
         In causal inference, you weight because you want a different parameter.
         Different question. Different reason.


     ATE, ATT, ATU are all expected values. But which expectation? Over which units? With what
     weights? That’s not a survey-design question — it’s a parameter-of-interest question.

        What this means for DiD on aggregate data


     DiD usually runs on aggregates: counties, states, hospitals.

         Equal weights per unit ⇒ unit-weighted ATE: the effect for the average location.
         Weight by population ⇒ person-weighted ATE: the effect for the average person.


      Which one you want depends on the question

      Person-weighted for aggregate human welfare: how many people are affected, by how much?
      Unit-weighted when locations have autonomy and goals: each policy unit chooses
      independently — the unit is the agent.


     Solon, Haider & Wooldridge (2015). Code: decks/code/Tiebout-Roy/tiebout_roy_chart.R

                             Yi = Di · Yi (1) + (1 − Di ) · Yi (0)

         This is all we ever see in the data: the realized half. The counterfactual half is forever missing.

        Calculations alone aren’t enough


         The SDO is something we can always calculate from the data.
         It is the population estimand of the naive comparison.
         But it is not the target parameter — in general, SDO ̸= ATT ̸= ATE ̸= ATU.
         The gap is the selection bias. It does not go away by collecting more data.



      To turn a calculation into a causal target, we need an identification strategy

      RCT: random assignment makes selection bias zero.
      IV: relevance + exclusion.
      RDD: continuity at the cutoff.
      DiD: parallel trends + no anticipation + stability (SUTVA).

              4. Standard Errors
                 Vanilla, Robust, Cluster-Robust
        The point estimate can be unbiased — the standard error often is not




         OLS gives us a β̂ that converges to the right thing under PT + NA + SUTVA
         But the spread of β̂ across hypothetical samples — the standard error — depends on the error
         structure
         Wrong SE → wrong t-statistic → wrong p-value → wrong conclusion


     Three problems live here: heteroskedasticity, within-cluster correlation, and serial correlation.




University of Zurich · Department of Economics · May 2026                                                78
        Three flavors of standard error




         Vanilla (homoskedastic): assumes Var(εi | Xi ) = σ 2 for all i
         Robust (Eicker-Huber-White): lets each residual speak for itself — handles heteroskedasticity
         Cluster-robust (CRVE): groups residuals by cluster, lets within-cluster errors correlate freely


     Same point estimate, three different SEs. The SE you pick is a claim about the error structure.




University of Zurich · Department of Economics · May 2026                                                  79
        Heteroskedasticity: variance changes with X




         Homoskedastic: same error variance everywhere — one σ 2 summarizes all observations
         Heteroskedastic: variance grows or shrinks with X (fan-shaped scatter is the classic picture)
         Vanilla SE pools every ê2i with equal weight → underestimates variance where X is extreme
         Robust SE weights each ê2i by that observation’s leverage

        Live: what should the t-statistic look like?




         Set up a placebo panel: true effect = 0. Run 500 times.
         For each run: estimate β̂ (unbiased, ≈ 0) and compute three SEs.

         Under correct inference, t should be distributed N (0, 1) — ≈ 5% in each tail beyond ±1.96.
         If SE is too small → histogram wider than N (0, 1) → over-rejection.

              7. Event Studies
                 as Falsifications — and 2×2s
        DiD is a courtroom case




       Scientific theories achieve credibility not just by explaining observed
       phenomena, but by excluding alternative explanations.
       — Scott Cunningham, The Remix (forthcoming, Yale 2026)


         You are the prosecutor. The DiD coefficient is your claim of guilt.
         A claim is not evidence. The evidence is everything you do around the claim.
         Popper: a theory earns credibility by deriving what else should be true if it’s right — and testing
         those predictions.




University of Zurich · Department of Economics · May 2026                                                      101
        Five parts of a strong DiD




      1. Bite — the policy actually changed what it was supposed to change
      2. Event study — pre/post coefficients around treatment
      3. Falsification — run a rival theory on a placebo group or outcome and try to reject it
      4. Main result — the DiD on the actual outcome
      5. Mechanism — the causal pathway from D to Y


     The point estimate alone is a case “so weak that readers and the public are justified to reject it.”




University of Zurich · Department of Economics · May 2026                                                   102
        A falsification puts a rival theory on trial


     Write down what else the rival theory would predict — something irrelevant to your
     treatment — and test it.

         Same outcome, alternative groups. A group your treatment shouldn’t reach, but the rival
         would.
         Min wage on low-wage employment? Re-run on higher-wage workers.
         Same group, alternative outcomes. Outcomes your treatment shouldn’t move, but the rival
         would.
         Cigarette tax on smoking-related hospitalizations? Re-run on appendicitis and fractures.


     Failing to reject the rival’s prediction weakens the rival — it never confirms you. That’s the Popperian
     asymmetry.



University of Zurich · Department of Economics · May 2026                                                       103
        A clean falsification: peer effects on height



         Christakis & Fowler (2007): peer-effect contagion in obesity
         Cohen-Cole & Fletcher (2008): re-run the same model on height, acne, and headaches
         All three show “contagion”




       We have not been able to become taller by having tall friends.
       — Cohen-Cole & Fletcher, paraphrased

     The result isn’t necessarily wrong. But the empirical model is suspect.




University of Zurich · Department of Economics · May 2026                                     104
        The event study is the falsification built into the timeline




         Every coefficient at τ = −2, −3, . . . , −q asks the same question:
         Is something already moving the treated group relative to controls, before D turned on?
         That’s a rival theory. The pre-period coefficients are its placebo test.
         This one is built into the regression. The others (placebo group, placebo outcome) you have to
         construct.

     Scott’s stronger claim: falsifications are more important than event studies for assessing parallel
     trends.
